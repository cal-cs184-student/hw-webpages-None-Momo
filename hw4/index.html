Overview

In this assignment I implemented a real-time cloth simulator using a mass-spring system. The cloth is discretized as a grid of point masses connected by three types of springs (structural, shearing, bending). At each time step, external forces and spring forces are accumulated, Verlet integration advances the positions, external collisions (sphere, plane) and self-collisions (spatial hashing) are resolved, and a Provot constraint prevents springs from stretching more than 10% per step. The renderer uses GLSL shaders to produce diffuse, Blinn-Phong, texture, bump-mapping, displacement-mapping, environment-mapped reflection, and a custom wind effect.

An interesting takeaway for me was how simulation and rendering interact: changing physical parameters like ks, density, and damping strongly changes motion dynamics and fold formation, while shader choices can dramatically alter perceived material detail even when geometry is unchanged.

Part 1: Masses and springs

The cloth grid is built in Cloth::buildGrid(). Point masses are laid out in row-major order with spacing width/(num_width_points−1) and height/(num_height_points−1). For a horizontal orientation the cloth lies in the xz-plane at y = 1; for vertical orientation it lies in the xy-plane with a small random z perturbation (∈ [−0.001, 0.001]) to break symmetry. Each point mass is checked against the pinned list loaded from the scene JSON.

After placing all point masses, springs are added in a single nested loop:

  • Structural — connects each point to the neighbor directly to the left and directly above.
  • Shearing — connects each point to the upper-left and upper-right diagonal neighbors.
  • Bending — connects each point to the neighbor two steps left and two steps above, resisting fold with a factor of 0.2× the spring constant.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Each simulation step proceeds in three stages:

  1. Force accumulation. All point masses start with the external force F_ext = mass × Σ(accelerations) (gravity etc.). Then Hooke's law is applied for every enabled spring: F = ks × (|ab| − L₀) × â, added to pm_a and subtracted from pm_b. Bending springs use 0.2 × ks.
  2. Verlet integration. \( x_{t+dt} = x_t + (1-d)(x_t - x_{t-dt}) + \frac{F}{m} dt^2 \), where d = damping/100. Pinned masses are skipped.
  3. Provot constraint [1995]. Any spring longer than 1.1× its rest length is corrected by moving both free endpoints toward each other (or the one free endpoint if the other is pinned) so the length equals exactly 1.1× rest length.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

After each Verlet integration step, every point mass is tested against all collision objects.

Sphere: If a point mass moves inside the sphere (|pm − origin| < radius), it is pushed back to the sphere surface. The tangent point is origin + radius × normalize(pm − origin). The corrected position is last_position + (1 − friction) × (tangent − last_position).

Plane: A crossing is detected when the signed distances from the plane change sign (front-to-back). The tangent point is the projection onto the plane plus SURFACE_OFFSET = 0.0001 along the normal to prevent z-fighting. Friction is applied identically to the sphere case.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Naively checking all O(n²) pairs is too slow. Instead, a spatial hash map is rebuilt every step:

  1. hash_position(pos) divides space into 3D boxes of size (3w/num_w) × (3h/num_h) × max(w,h), truncates each coordinate to its box index, and combines them with large primes (1,000,003 / 1,000,033 / 1,000,037) to produce a float hash.
  2. build_spatial_map() inserts every point mass into the bucket corresponding to its hash.
  3. self_collide(pm) iterates only over candidates in the same bucket. For each candidate closer than 2 × thickness, a correction vector is accumulated. The average correction is divided by simulation_steps for stability.

Part 5: Shaders

What is a shader program?

A shader program is a pair of small GPU programs — a vertex shader and a fragment shader — that together control how 3D geometry is rendered. The vertex shader runs once per vertex, transforming positions to clip space and forwarding per-vertex data (position, normal, UV, tangent) as interpolatable out variables. The fragment shader runs once per rasterized pixel, receiving interpolated values and computing the final pixel color using lighting equations, texture lookups, and material effects. Together: geometry → vertex shader → rasterization → fragment shader → framebuffer.

Blinn-Phong shading model

Blinn-Phong decomposes reflected light into three terms:

  • Ambient — constant base: \( k_a \cdot I_a \)
  • Diffuse — Lambertian: \( k_d \cdot \frac{I}{r^2} \cdot \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}) \)
  • Specular — glossy highlight with half-vector \(\mathbf{h} = \text{normalize}(\mathbf{l}+\mathbf{v})\): \( k_s \cdot \frac{I}{r^2} \cdot \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{h})^p \)

Texture Mapping

Bump Mapping vs Displacement Mapping

Both shaders use a height map to simulate surface detail, but differ in where the perturbation applies:

  • Bump mapping only perturbs the surface normal used in lighting. Vertex positions are unchanged. The TBN matrix transforms the height-map gradient into world-space: dU = (h(uv+[1/W,0]) − h(uv)) × kn × kh, yielding a perturbed normal.
  • Displacement mapping physically moves each vertex along its normal by h(uv) × kh in the vertex shader, then applies Bump-style lighting in the fragment shader. This changes silhouettes and casts real geometric shadows — something bump mapping cannot do.

Mirror / Environment-Mapped Reflections

The mirror shader reflects the incident view ray around the surface normal: wi = reflect(normalize(pos − cam), n), then samples the cube map texture(u_texture_cubemap, wi).

(Optional) Part 6: Extra Credit — Wind Simulation

I implemented a spatially-varying wind force. Three fields were added to ClothParameters: enable_wind, wind_strength, and wind_direction. In Cloth::simulate(), after applying gravity, a wind loop runs over all free point masses:

  1. Spatial variation: a sine wave modulates local strength — wave = sin(4x + 2y), giving local_strength = F × (0.7 + 0.3 × wave). This produces a rippling wave across the cloth rather than uniform pressure.
  2. Face-normal filtering: wind only pushes faces that face into the wind. facing = dot(n, wind_dir) — force is only applied where facing > 0, proportional to the projected area, mimicking real aerodynamic pressure.

A dedicated Wind GUI panel was added to clothSimulator.cpp with a toggle button, force strength slider, and direction (x/y/z) inputs — all editable at runtime.
